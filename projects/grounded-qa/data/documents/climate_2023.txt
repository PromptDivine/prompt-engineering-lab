TITLE: Record Global Temperatures and Climate Consequences in 2023
SOURCE: Climate Research Digest
DATE: 2024-01-15

Global average temperatures in 2023 reached 1.45 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels, the highest ever recorded and approaching the critical 1.5-degree threshold set by the Paris Agreement, according to the European Union's Copernicus Climate Change Service. Every month of 2023 set a new monthly temperature record, a streak that continued into early 2024.

Scientists attribute the record heat to the combination of long-term warming driven by greenhouse gas emissions and a strong El Niño weather pattern in the Pacific Ocean. El Niño typically adds 0.1 to 0.2 degrees Celsius of additional warming on top of the underlying trend.

The consequences were felt worldwide. Canada experienced its worst wildfire season on record, destroying more than 18 million hectares of forest — more than double the previous record set in 1995. The fires released an estimated 647 megatons of carbon dioxide, further accelerating warming in a feedback loop that concerns scientists.

Mediterranean Europe and North Africa endured extended heat waves with temperatures exceeding 45 degrees Celsius in some locations. Greece, Italy, and Tunisia recorded their hottest days since records began. The heat waves were linked to approximately 60,000 excess deaths across Europe during the summer months.

Sea surface temperatures also hit unprecedented highs, averaging 0.9 degrees Celsius above the 1982-2011 baseline. This threatens coral reef systems globally — the Great Barrier Reef experienced its fourth mass bleaching event in seven years. Disrupted fisheries affect billions of people who depend on ocean protein for food security.

Climate scientists warn that without dramatic reductions in carbon emissions within this decade, temporary breaches of the 1.5-degree threshold will become permanent. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change estimates that limiting warming to 1.5 degrees requires cutting global emissions by 43 percent by 2030 relative to 2019 levels.

Current national climate pledges, if fully implemented, would limit warming to approximately 2.5 degrees Celsius by 2100, according to the UN Environment Programme's annual Emissions Gap Report.
